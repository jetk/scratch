1. Value proposition

The challenge: According to 2014 data from the World Bank, 2 billion adults globally were unbanked such as people with low or unstable incomes, or who have experienced a significant life shock. Accenture says that this demographic is denied the fundamentals of economic empowerment. Being unbanked also comes at a price: retail purchases are more expensive than online shopping, paying per bill costs more than direct debit and non-bank remittances are quite costly.

The solution: Headquartered in London and launched in 2014, Comp-X provides an online account, a contactless MasterCard debit card and a mobile app to the unbanked and underserved with a vision to be the world’s most inclusive bank. Salaries and government benefits can be directly paid into Comp-X accounts. Cash can be deposited at 28,000 partner locations. Fees are economical, simple and transparent. The service is fully automated, quick and straight forward. Comp-X’s roadmap includes further services such as sending remittances abroad, overdrafts, home and car insurance, credit score builders, micro loans and credit cards.

 

2. Market size, trends and drivers

The Financial Inclusion Commission says that 50% of the 1.5 million unbanked adults in the UK would like to use banking services. An additional 9 million customers have access only to basic bank accounts. Although many of those people live in areas without bank branches, 40% of branches had been closed between 1989 and 2012 predominantly in low income areas, most of them could easily sign up with Comp-X because smartphone penetration is 61% even in the DE socioeconomic segments (UK average is 71%). Comp-X estimates that over the next 4 years approximately 40% of this target market would become active banking customers each generating a revenue of £55-60 p.a. This would create a £210-£230 million market, of which Comp-X plans to capture 9%.

 

3. Competitive landscape and positioning

Comp-X is focused on a digital-first strategy targeting low-income customers with a simple sign-up and transaction process, along with low and transparent pricing. The Company had signed up XXX,000 users by Nov 2016 and is expecting to increase its user base to XXX,000 by EOY2018. Comp-X is facing competition from various angles:

High street and traditional challenger banks (e.g. Barclays, TSB, Metro): Historically, established banks have found it difficult to service this target market due to (i) its relative low revenue potential; (ii) limited availability of KYC information, and (iii) the average banks’ high operating costs. Then in December 2014 the 9 largest banks agreed with the UK government to roll out free basic accounts to the financially disenfranchised, starting in Jan 2016. However, given their high cost base, it is not likely that those banks would actively solicit business to Comp-X’s target market. Combining this with the established banks’ complex processes, legacy systems and slow pace, Pocket believes that the Company would be able to fend off this threat. Their view has been proven by Comp-X’s ability to almost triple their monthly customer acquisition from 3,000 in Jan 2016 to 8,000 in Nov 2016.

Large retailers offering banking services (e.g. Tesco, ASDA): Although most of those competitors rely on the infrastructure of traditional banks, they have various cost advantages over banks due to their extensive footprint, lower cost locations and broader product set to spread their marketing expenditure. They also have stronger brands and higher customer satisfaction, better m-commerce channels and longer experience with low income customers than banks. Nevertheless, large retailers tend to target higher income customers with their financial products. Even, ASDA, the discount retailer, still does not offer debit cards and simple bank accounts.

Digital challenger banks (e.g. Atom, Loot, Monese): Although some of them appear to be similar to Comp-X in terms of current product offering and transparent pricing, none of them focuses on Comp-X’s target market. Most digital banks are in an earlier stage of their development giving Comp-X the opportunity to build its brand and market share. While Comp-X operates under an e-money license and heavily rely on the infrastructure of external partners, other digital challengers use banking licenses and have built their own IT infrastructure, which make Comp-X much more cost and capital efficient.

 

4. Business model

A substantial part of Comp-X’s operations has been outsourced to reputable external partners which are linked together through a proprietary platform. It is entirely cloud-based, running from Amsterdam and Dublin data centres, hosted in Provider A. Basic tenets of platform are performance, scale, redundancy and flexibility. Comp-X’s development teams are based in the UK and Poland. External partners include Provider B providing custody and sort-code. Provider C has the e-money license and provides settlement to Provider D. Provider E processes transactions and Provider F runs KYC. Cyber security penetration testing is provided by a 3rd party supplier. Product development is driven by customers through continuous monitoring of the activities of 5,000 users. For each product Comp-X assesses the best way to build or partner. For example, credit products will involve partnering with no risk on balance sheet. Low-cost social media and ‘guerilla’ marketing channels are used for customer acquisition such as Facebook advertising, affiliates, direct response advertising in local papers, community marketing, refer-a-friend and partnerships. For example, Comp-X is just about to partner up with the Department of Work and Pensions for the distribution of monthly benefits. The revenue model includes (i) 99p charges for 5 basic services (e.g. ATM cash withdrawals); (ii) currency conversion; (iii) rebates from merchants, and; (iv) affiliate relationships with retailers. The most important cash deposit channel is Provider G’s nationwide network of 28,000 outlets. KYC/AML is fully automated. Account opening checks are performed by Provider H while transactions are monitored by Comp-X’s proprietary tool. Any suspicious activity is reported to Provider I and passed on to the FCA.
